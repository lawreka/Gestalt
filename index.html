How to use the Figure-Ground Principle in design
What is the Figure-Ground Principle?

The Figure must be different enough from the Ground to be perceived as its own object *
Remember the Figure-Ground Principle to make sure the elements of your design are clear and functional.
Avoid the problems illustrated in the three examples below (scroll for full examples).

Problem 1

Before

Due to insufficient contrast between figure and ground, the text in the magazine's subtitle is lost.

After

Text color can be changed from blue to white for more contrast, so that small text stands out.

Problem 2

Before

The edges of the product figures blur into the background and the figures are lost.

After

The white ground could be extended past the edges of the figures so they do not disappear. This solution creates other problems in the original design of the ad, but the figure-ground problem is solved.

Problem 3

Before

The figure and ground are confused because of the two patterns. The first problem is that they are both too similar and merge into an all-grey image.

After

The second problem is that the pattern on the guitar-shaped figure makes it look transparent which is confusing. There are very few cases where a transparent figure will make the image more clear, so in general this effect is to be avoided.
